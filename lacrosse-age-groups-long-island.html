Youth Lacrosse
Age Groups Explained
— Long Island

🥍 Lacrosse Parent Guide 8 min read · Updated 2025

If you're new to youth lacrosse on Long Island, the age group system can be confusing. PAL leagues use one format. Travel clubs use another. Some programs list "U13" and others list "2030." This guide breaks it all down so you know exactly where your child belongs and what to expect at each level.

Two Different Systems

Youth lacrosse on Long Island operates under two different age classification systems depending on the type of program:

  • Under (U) format: Used by PAL recreational leagues and US Lacrosse for youth divisions. U9, U11, U13, U15 are the standard divisions.
  • Graduation year format: Used by travel clubs like Team 91, Express Lacrosse, LI Empire, and Rebels LC. A "2030" team means players graduating high school in 2030.

Quick conversion: To find your child's graduation year, take the current year, subtract their current grade from 12, and add the result. Example: 8th grader in 2025 → 12 - 8 = 4 → 2025 + 4 = Class of 2029.

Boys vs. Girls Differences

Boys and girls lacrosse are different sports with different rules, equipment, and physical demands at each age level. This affects age group expectations significantly.

Boys Lacrosse

Full contact begins at the U13/7th grade level. Boys programs at U9 and U11 emphasize stick skills and small-sided games. By U13, players are learning positional assignments, face-offs, and full-field play. Full equipment — helmet, shoulder pads, arm pads, gloves, and mouth guard — is required at all levels.

Girls Lacrosse

Girls lacrosse is a non-contact sport at all youth levels. Equipment requirements are lighter — goggles and a mouth guard are standard; helmets are not required for field players. Girls programs often start developing college-ready skills earlier in the recruiting timeline because the D1 women's game moves faster relative to physical development.

When Should My Child Start Travel Lacrosse?

This is the most common question from Long Island lacrosse parents. The honest answer: it depends on your child — not on what everyone else is doing.

Most Long Island travel clubs start accepting players at the U11/2033–2034 level (4th–5th grade). Some elite programs like Team 91 and Express run developmental camps even earlier, but formal travel team commitment before 4th grade is generally not recommended.

The best time to start travel lacrosse is when:

  • Your child is asking to play more — not just going along with it
  • They've played at least one PAL season and understand basic rules and positions
  • Your family can commit to the schedule — typically 2 practices and weekend games during the season
  • The cost is manageable without significant financial stress

Don't panic about being "behind": Long Island lacrosse culture can feel like a race to start travel as early as possible. In reality, many D1 college players didn't start serious travel lacrosse until 6th or 7th grade. Development matters more than which team your 9-year-old played for.

When Does College Recruiting Start?

College lacrosse recruiting on Long Island begins earlier than most parents expect. For D1 programs specifically:

  • 9th–10th grade (2028–2029): Top players begin attending college showcases and getting noticed by coaches.
  • 10th grade (2028): D1 coaches can begin official contact. Many top LI players are verbally committed by the end of sophomore year.
  • 11th grade (2027): Official visits begin. Most D1 rosters are nearly full by end of junior year.
  • 12th grade (2026): D2, D3, and NAIA programs are still recruiting actively.

Long Island Programs by Age Group

Here's a quick guide to what type of program fits each age group on LI:

  • K–3rd grade: Nassau/Suffolk PAL clinics and recreational leagues. No travel needed.
  • 3rd–5th grade: PAL league games + optional travel club development programs.
  • 5th–7th grade: Primary travel club entry point. Try Team 91, Express, LI Empire, LI Jesters, Rebels LC.
  • 7th–9th grade: Full travel commitment. Beginning college recruiting awareness.
  • 9th–12th grade: High school + club. LILJ Winter League. Active recruiting process.
